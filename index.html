Project Details

Yoglo

User Experience Design

Year:

Fall. 2018

Role(s):

User Experience Designer User Experience Researcher

Duration:

10 weeks


Introduction

In the past 4 years, Seattle has seen a 700% increase in the yoga community. With such a huge inflation, my team felt that this was the perfect opportunity to apply our knowledge of the user-centered design process to new yoga practitioners.

Yoglo is a smart mat supplemented by an app, designed with the new student in mind. The aim is to provide the user with information about correct hand and feet placement through an ambient display of lights.


Research

To begin the project my team of 3 reached out to several yoga studios around Seattle asking if we could host interviews. We interviewed a couple instructors, a few experienced "yogis", and a few beginners. Each interview gave us interesting insights into some of the pain points that exist within the yoga community.


Pain Points

1. Troublesome having to carry big yoga mat and other equipment around, can get heavy.

2. Yoga experience relied heavily on an instructor/app to guide them through the sequences.

3. Going to studio can be intimidating because there are people of all skill levels.

4. Sometimes hard to remember all the different yoga poses. Want to make sure posture is correct.

5. Having a partner to practice with would make the experience more enjoyable.


Deliverables

Our team put together two personas; One that embodied a relatively new yoga practitioner and one that represented a yoga instructor. We then created a user journey map for our yoga practitioner Sara, helping us contextualize her actions, thoughts, emotions throughout her yoga experience. This helped identify points of interest that we could potentially design for.



Personas representing a yoga instructor (top) and yoga practitioner (bottom).




User journey map where tracked Sara's emotions: Enthusiasm, Confidence, Relaxation.


Problem Statement

"How might we use technology to improve yoga pose knowledge, while allowing the user to maintain a mindful state?"



Ideation

Before we could start the ideation process our team identified design requirements that addressed our problem statement. Our solution should...


Design Requirements

1. Help communicate correct body position for various poses.

2. Help user connect with yoga community and build relationships.

3. Promote mindfulness and body awareness.

4. Provide feedback to support learning and growth.


Storyboarding

Now with a clear set of guidelines to design around we could start generating solutions in the form of sketched and photographed storyboards.



For each of our storyboards we meticulously considered the feasibility and functionality of our solutions. We came to the conclusion that we wanted to create a smart mat that ambiently (using lights) conveyed to the user how to mimic poses correctly. We also realized we would have to supplement our mat with a app in order for the mat to know what pose to guide the user through.


Information Architecture

To better understand how we wanted our mat + app combo to work we created an information architecture to map out the different pathways users could interact with our interfaces.



The main takeaway here is that users have an option to create or follow predetermined yoga flows depending on whether they are practicing at home or at a studio. Users can then download these flows onto the mat, which then guides them through the flow.


Prototyping

Our group decided to create low-fidelity paper prototypes because they're cheap and easy to modify. Our prototypes were based around three tasks that we wanted users to complete:

Task 1. Create a custom yoga flow from home.

Task 2. Pick a predetermined class flow from a studio.

Task 3. Follow the yoga flow displayed by the mat.




Takeaways

After conducting some usability tests, our users were happy with the functionality of the app, but concerned about the functionality of the mat. They were unsure when the mat would transition into the next pose and also thought the lights/display might become a distraction as they become more familiar with the poses. Our users suggested:

1. Allow practitioners to set a time limit for each yoga pose.

2. Add a slide bar on the mat to allow users to customize the light intensity.

3. Add a switch button to turn off and on the visual display on the mat.


Design

To begin the design process our team developed wireframes and schematics to better represent the actions a user can make while using our app and mat. The two main pathways a user can follow are: creating custom flows and selecting a studio class.


Wireframes/Schematics




High-Fidelity Mockups

Following several rounds of critique sessions, the final step in our design process culminated in three high-fidelity mockups of our app and mat. These design choices reflect feedback given to us throughout every phase of our research, ideation, and usability testing.


The Mat




Design Choices

We settled on a mat that used an ambient display of lights, to let the user know where to place their hands and feet during a specific pose.

1. LED lights (to help convey correct limb placement).

2. Display pad (to help user know what pose looks like).

3. Switch (allow user to turn on and off display).

4. Slide Bar (allow user to adjust light intensity).


The App




Design Choices

The studio interface allows users to search for specific classes offered at a studio of their choice. Creating a more personal experience, the user is able to find a class that fits their interest and skill level.

1. Search Filter (Allows user to search by class or instructor).

2. Community Tab (Lets user interact with that specific yoga community).



Design Choices

The progress report interface, highlights some of the user's stats. The main feature of this page is the balance tab, another alternative form of feedback that lets the user know how well they were able to maintain a posture.

1. Past Flows Tab (Allows user to compare and contrast their progress).

2. Swivel Circle (Helps convey to user that they must swipe in order to rotate between poses).


Reflection

Although constrained to 10 weeks, I learned a lot about the user-centered design process. Early on in the project I just wanted to design, design, design. However, I quickly realized you can't design anything if you don't know anything about your user. In order to have a successful design I had to do my research first, test my ideas, and then build off feedback I received. Context is everything. One key takeaway that was driven home to me by my professor was to eliminate my design biases so that I don't design around my needs. This is part of the reason why my group decided to design for yoga practitioners. None of us had any prior knowledge with yoga, so we couldn't assume things about our users' pain points.

If I ever have more time to go back and work on this project I would love to focus more on the community aspect of our design. The yoga instructor we talked to mentioned that, "even though the yoga community is growing, it is still homogeneous and lacks diversity."

BECU

User Interface Design

Year:

Winter. 2019

Role(s):

User Interface Designer

Duration:

2 week


Introduction

BECU or Boeing Employees' Credit Union, is a financial institution established in 1935 to serve past and current Boeing employees. Since then, BECU has expanded to serve the general population and has made strides to develop various online banking applications.

For this two week assignment the purpose was to evaluate the effectiveness and functionality of a user interface and develop an improved version for it. One interface I often use and sometimes have difficulty navigating is BECU's online banking platform. For this reason I thought it would be the perfect opportunity to redesign the layout.


Research

Even though this assignment was mainly geared towards redesigning interfaces that I struggle with I still wanted to make sure that I wasn't the only person who found BECU's online banking interface frustrating. With a quick Google search I found a fair bit of criticism towards BECU's interface. One anonymous Reddit user said, "The UI is horrendous and is different on every browser."

Knowing that others were also unsatisfied with BECU's interface it created an interesting problem space to work with. I then developed the following three criteria which I used to evaluate the effectiveness of interface.


Criteria

1. How well it prevents errors.

2. How Accessible it is.

3. How well it promotes Awareness (informs user of virtual location).


Using the criteria from above I evaluated two interfaces from BECU's online banking website (home page and bill payment screens).



Home page interface




Bill payments interface


Findings

1. BECU does a good job preventing user errors by implementing multiple confirmation screens and allowing users to quickly edit a mistake.

2. Not very accessible as text is small and the screen are cluttered with an excessive number of tabs. It is also hard for users to get immediate assistance if needed.

3. Lack of clear hierarchy makes it hard for the user to know what they are looking at.


Ideation

Using the findings I came up with three things I wanted to improve upon in my redesign.


Design Requirements

1. Implement a live chat for immediate access to help.

2. Optimize the menu to help reduce clutter and reduce confusion.

3. Enlarge text and create more contrast when highlighting between selected tabs.


Design

Below are the final mockups I created for the two interfaces. I wanted to preserve the same feel of the original designs but just clean up the layout.


Design Choices

1. Implemented a live chat menu on the bottom left corner of the screen. Screen is minimized to reduce clutter.

2. Got rid of top two rows of tabs as they were not immediately applicable to online banking experience.

3. Created more visual hierarchy by adding a red bar under the tab users are currently on.

4. Enlarged text



Redesigned home page interface




Redesigned home page interface

Reflection

Although this project was severely time constrained I still learned a valuable skill. I came away with a better understanding of how to critically analyze the effectiveness of an interface. In all honesty, now it's hard for me to visit any website without carefully examining the UI. If I had more time I would've liked to have conducted more research in order to figure out more pain points users have with BECU's interface.

This was also a great way to practice my design skills!

Seafair v Aquarium

Visual Communication Design

Year:

Spring. 2018

Role(s):

Graphic Designer

Duration:

3 week


Objective

The mission of this project was to design posters for two competing events that took place during the same time. The posters were supposed to visually communicate the emotions associated with each event so that civilians would have an easier time deciding which one to attend.

For my two events I decided to keep things local to my hometown and design for the Seafair festival and an visit to the Seattle Aquarium.


Brainstorming

Before I started designing anything I took the initiative to start brainstorming first. For each event I thought about the emotions they evoke and the feelings I get when I envision myself there.


Seafair

Seafair is a family friendly festival that takes place during summertime. It hosts a variety of exhilarating events including: Military air shows, hydroplane racing, wakeboarding, and an invasion of pirates! Based on this context I came up with three emotions I wanted to convey. The poster should feel...

Festive, Exciting, and Inviting



Aquarium

Contrary to Seafair, the aquarium is a much more mellow, kid-friendly environment. Being immersed in the aquatic world is captivates our attention and encourages learning and exploration. Based on this context I developed three more emotions in which the poster should feel...

Adventurous, Mysterious, and Playful



Inspiration

With a firm grasp of the emotions I wanted to convey I began looking for inspiration. I searched Pinterest and Google for previous posters made for these events, as well as posters that I felt gave off the vibes I was chasing after.




Collection of inspiration



Synthesis

Following the collection of ideas I gathered, I was able to develop some design requirements centered around three concepts of color, typography, and composition.


Seafair

1. Triadic color scheme (red, yellow, and blue) - to make poster feel warm and inviting.

2. Loud fonts - to help communicate the festiveness and exhilarating feel.

3. Dynamic composition - to help make the poster feel active and busy.


Aquarium

1. Analogous Color Scheme (blue) - to make poster feel mysterious and evoke curiosity.

2. Bubbly/handwritten fonts - to help communicate playfulness and feel kid-friendly.

3. Dynamic composition - to help convey activity and promote feelings of exploration.

Reflection

In most regards, this project was my introduction into the world of design. It taught me a lot about how color, typography, and various compositional techniques can be used to create emotions. One challenge I had to overcome was learning how to use Illustrator. Prior to this project I had zero experience working with any Adobe software. As a result, I spent many hours watching Youtube videos and following guides, doing my best to equip myself with as many tools possible.

Above all, I walked away from this project with a new confidence. I didn't have to be an expert in Adobe in order to produce creative results, I just had to seek help, ask questions, and most importantly, be willing to learn.

Wall.E

SolidWorks 3D Modeling

Year:

Fall. 2017

Role(s):

3D Computer Drafter

Duration:

3 week


Introduction

Wall-E was the culmination of my time in my ME 123 class. The objective of this project was to apply all the tools that we've learned throughout the quarter into one multi-part object. I decided to recreate one of Disney's most iconic characters, Wall-E. The reason I picked Wall-E is because his frame consists of very unique parts which allowed me to really demonstrate my skills.


Project Requirements

5 parts
Extrude (1)
Reference Plane (1)
Revolve (1)
Shell or Thin Feature (1)
Hole (1)
Rib (1)
Equation (1)
Feature Pattern (1)
3D Sweep (1)
Loft (1)



Research

To begin this project I had to conduct some research. I started out by Googling various blueprints and schematics for Wall-E so I could get a better understanding of his scale. During my research I found it hard to find blueprints that provided accurate dimensions, so I ended up having to eyeball some of his features.



Blueprints and Schematics for Wall-E



Design

Using the blueprints and schematics from my research above, I recreated Wall-E to the best of my abilities. I mainly focused on the isometric view (schematic in the bottom left corner) as it gave me a better idea of what each part looked like. Due to time constraints I had to simplify some parts, but in the end my Wall-E turned out just as depicted above. Although, the color could use some work...



High quality render of my project




Accolades

To my delight, my project received high praise from my professor and wound up as one of the top 10 projects in the class.



Reflection

This was probably one of my more memorable projects I've worked on thus far. I consider myself to be a very visual learner, so being just being able to make the images in my mind come to life was extremely gratifying. More than anything though, I came away from this project with a new prototyping skill. A skill that proved useful when I had to model a smart yoga mat for my project "Yoglo".
